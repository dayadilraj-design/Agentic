Planning: Task Decomposition & Multi-Step Reasoning

Simple tool-calling loops work for one-shot tasks. Real work -- 'plan a launch,' 'refactor this module' -- needs the agent to break a goal into an ordered set of sub-tasks first.

The moment a task requires more than one meaningful decision in sequence, you need a planning strategy, or the agent will thrash: calling tools in the wrong order, forgetting the overall goal, or declaring victory too early.

Three planning patterns, from simplest to most structured

  • Reactive (ReAct-style) -- no upfront plan; the agent decides one step at a time based on what it currently sees. Simple, flexible, but can wander on long tasks.
  • Plan-and-execute -- the agent first writes out an explicit numbered plan, then executes each step, optionally replanning if a step reveals new information.
  • Hierarchical decomposition -- a top-level planner breaks a goal into sub-goals, each of which may be handled by its own sub-agent or sub-loop (this is the bridge into Module 9's multi-agent systems).
When to use which

Reactive is fine for short tasks (2-4 tool calls). Plan-and-execute earns its overhead once a task has enough steps that losing track of the goal becomes a real risk. Hierarchical decomposition is worth the complexity only when sub-tasks are genuinely independent enough to benefit from separate context.

For managers

If an agent's task takes more than a few steps, ask to see its plan before it starts executing, not just its final output. A visible plan is one of the cheapest oversight mechanisms available, and it's a natural point for a human to catch a bad approach early.

Here's a plan-and-execute skeleton, the most common upgrade from a purely reactive loop.

# Step 1: ask the model to produce an explicit plan first
plan_response = model.call(
    system="You are a planning assistant. Break the user's goal into a "
           "numbered list of concrete sub-tasks. Do not execute anything yet.",
    messages=[{"role": "user", "content": goal}]
)
plan = parse_plan(plan_response.text)  # e.g. ["Research competitors", "Draft outline", "Write intro"]

# Step 2: execute each step, feeding results forward
completed_steps = []
for step in plan:
    result = run_agent_step(step, context=completed_steps)
    completed_steps.append({"step": step, "result": result})

    # Step 3: optional replanning if a step reveals the plan needs to change
    if result.get("requires_replan"):
        plan = replan(goal, completed_steps)

Why explicit plans help debugging

A written plan is a checkpoint you can inspect, log, and even show to a human for approval before any tool with real-world side effects runs -- turning a black-box multi-step task into something auditable.

Exercise 6.1 -- Choose a planning strategy

Match the task to the right planning pattern

  1. "What's 15% of $340?"
  2. "Research three competitor pricing pages and summarize the differences."
  3. "Plan and execute our entire quarterly content calendar across blog, social, and email."
Show suggested answers

1. Reactive -- a single tool call (a calculator), no real planning needed.
2. Plan-and-execute -- a handful of clearly ordered steps (visit each page, extract data, compare) benefit from an explicit plan.
3. Hierarchical decomposition -- three genuinely independent workstreams (blog, social, email) are natural candidates for separate sub-agents coordinated by a top-level planner.
